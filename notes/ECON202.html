Economics - the study of how scarce resources, which have alternative uses, are allocated amongst comp

3 questions of economics

  1. What to produce and what quantities?
  2. How to produce?
  3. For whom are good produced?

Production of possibility curve (PPC)

Check notes for graph example

Trade:

Advantages to trade:

Trades are typically mutually beneficial.

  • Value is created as goods are channeled to those who desire the most.
  • EX: Suppose chris bought a dvd for $20. If chris was willing to pay at most $35. and it cos the seller $15 to supply the dvd. How much value is created?
    • Seller makes 20-15 = $5 profit; $35 is the true value of the DVD to Chris. $35-20 = $15. $5 + $15 = $20 - consumer surplus
  • More goods can be produced through division of labor, learning by doing, and the adoption of mass production technology.
  • More output can be produced and received through specialization.

Disadvantages of trade

Destruction of high opportunity cost industries

reliable on trading partners for important goods

Specialization in some goods may be worse than others for long term growth

More potential problems with border “issues” (drugs, etc)

Differences in each economys “rules” (labor/environmental laws, tax policy, etc) may cause specialization unrelated to comparative advantage

Chapter 7:

GDP - A measure of output

The gross domestic product (GDP) is the market value of final goods and services produced within a country during a specific time period, usually a year.

Rules for calculating gdp:

  • Only final goods and services count.
  • Only transactions involving production count
  • Only production within the country is counted
  • Only goods produced within the current period are counted.

Personal consumtion purchases are the largest component to gdp.

Expenditure approach:

  • Personal consumption
  • gross private investment
  • government consumption and gross investment
  • net exports

Law of demand:

All else equal, there is a negative relationship between the price of a good and the quantity demanded by consumers.

Law of supply:

All else equal, there is a positive relationship between the price of a good and the quantity supplied by sellers.

Shifts in demand:

Whenever people are willing to by more/less at any given price

Causes in shifts of demand

  • changes in consumer incomes.
  • changes in the price of related goods
    • Substitutes: cars and motorcycles
    • Compliments: cars and gasoline.
  • changes in consumer tastes
  • changes in the number of potential consumers:
    • immigration
    • trade policy

Shifts in supply:

Change in input costs (labor, raw materils, etc)

Change in regulations/laws

Changes in taxes on sellers (sales tax, excise taxes)

Changes in production technologies

What doesn GDP measure

gdp omits the value of household production (cleaning house, making meals, taking care of kids)

gdp omits the production done in the underground economy (illegal drugs, “under the table work”, unrecorded cash or barter transactions.)

gdp omits the value of lesiure

gdp doesn’t subtract the value of economic “bads” produced (pollutin, traffic, stress)

Calculating GDP:

Value added approach: sum of revenues - intermediate good costs for all firms.

Resource cost / income approach: sum of the resource payments - intermediate goods cost.
